## 1. Story Framework

### The World

**Year**: 1943. The war rages on. Europe bleeds under the iron boot of the Third Reich. From the frozen Eastern Front to the burning deserts of North Africa, the Allies fight for survival. But beneath the surface of conventional warfare, a shadow war is being fought -- one of intelligence, sabotage, and assassination.

The **Office of Secret Actions (OSA)**, a covert branch of Allied intelligence, wages this invisible war. Their agents are ghosts. They infiltrate, they extract, they eliminate. And their best operative -- the one they send when the mission is suicide and failure is not an option -- is a man the Nazis have learned to fear.

His name is **William Joseph Blazkowicz**.

### The Man

B.J. Blazkowicz is not a spy in the James Bond mold. He is a Polish-American from Milwaukee, the grandson of immigrants, broad-shouldered and blunt-jawed, built like the steel mill workers he grew up with. He enlisted the day after Pearl Harbor. He fought in North Africa. He was recruited by the OSA not for his subtlety but for his extraordinary capacity to survive the unsurvivable.

He is quiet. He is direct. When he speaks, he speaks simply. But behind those steady blue eyes burns something the Nazis never accounted for: a fury that does not dim, does not tire, does not stop. He has seen what they do to the villages, to the prisoners, to the people who cannot fight back. And he has made a promise that he will keep making them pay until there are none left.

### The Promise

This is not a story about glory. It is a story about one man descending into escalating horrors -- from a medieval dungeon to a mad scientist's laboratory to the personal bunker of Adolf Hitler himself -- and emerging, bloodied but unbroken, every single time.

Each episode raises the stakes. Each episode reveals a deeper layer of Nazi depravity. And B.J. carries it all, absorbs it all, and keeps moving forward.

Because someone has to.

### Episode 1: Escape from Castle Wolfenstein

**Setting**: Castle Wolfenstein, deep in the Bavarian Alps. A medieval fortress -- 800 years of stone and shadow -- repurposed by the SS as a high-security prison for captured Allied operatives. Its walls are ten feet thick. Its dungeons extend three levels beneath the earth. No one has ever escaped.

**Story**:

B.J.'s mission was supposed to be reconnaissance -- infiltrate the castle, photograph the garrison strength, exfiltrate. But a local informant was a double agent. B.J. walked into a trap. He was beaten, stripped of his weapons and equipment, and thrown into the deepest cell in the castle.

That was two weeks ago.

For fourteen days, B.J. has endured interrogation. SS-Obersturmbannfuhrer Hans Grosse has overseen the sessions personally. But B.J. has given them nothing. Not his name. Not his unit. Not a single word. Grosse is losing patience. Tomorrow, the interrogation moves to "final methods."

Tonight, a single guard brings B.J. his last meal. The guard is young, barely twenty, and he makes the mistake of turning his back. It will be the last mistake he ever makes.

**Level Progression**:
- **Floor 1 (Levels 1-2)**: The dungeon. Dark, wet, claustrophobic. Stone walls, iron doors, the sound of dripping water and distant screams. B.J. has only a knife and a pistol with limited ammo. Every bullet counts. Enemies are sparse -- guards, attack dogs.
- **Floor 2 (Levels 3-5)**: The lower castle. Barracks, armories, kitchens. More guards, tighter patrols. B.J. acquires the machine gun. The pace shifts from survival horror to action.
- **Floor 3 (Levels 6-8)**: The upper castle. Officers' quarters, communications rooms, the main gate. SS officers appear -- tougher, faster, better armed. The castle is on alert now. B.J. fights through a wall of resistance.
- **Floor 4 (Levels 9-10)**: Hans Grosse's domain. The warden's private wing. Ornate halls soaked in Nazi iconography. The approach to the final confrontation.

**Boss**: Hans Grosse

**Tone**: Desperate survival escalating to explosive escape. The early levels are slow, tense, almost horror-like. The player is weak, ammo is scarce, and every encounter feels dangerous. As B.J. acquires better weapons and builds momentum, the tone shifts to action -- but never loses the edge of desperation. This is a man fighting for his life in the belly of the beast.

**Cinematic Intro**:
*Fade from black. The sound of water dripping on stone. A single bare bulb swings from the ceiling, casting swaying shadows across a medieval cell. B.J. sits on the floor, back against the wall, wrists raw from chains. His face is bruised, one eye swollen. But his expression is calm. Calculating.*

*The cell door scrapes open. A young guard enters carrying a tin plate -- grey bread, thin soup. He sets it on the floor, glances at the prisoner. B.J. doesn't move. The guard relaxes -- just another broken man. He turns to leave.*

*B.J.'s hand shoots forward.*

*SMASH CUT TO BLACK.*

*Title card: EPISODE 1 -- ESCAPE FROM CASTLE WOLFENSTEIN*

**Cinematic Outro**:
*B.J. emerges from the castle's main gate into the cold Bavarian night. Behind him, the castle burns -- alarms wailing, searchlights sweeping wildly, smoke pouring from the windows of Hans Grosse's shattered domain. B.J. pauses at the tree line, breathing hard, blood on his hands and uniform. He looks back at the castle one last time.*

*He doesn't smile. He just turns and disappears into the dark forest.*

*Text overlay: "Blazkowicz escaped Castle Wolfenstein. But what he found there -- the documents, the whispers of something called 'Eisenfaust' -- would send him somewhere far worse."*

### Episode 2: Operation Eisenfaust

**Setting**: A sprawling underground laboratory complex beneath a bombed-out hospital in the German countryside. What the locals think is a field hospital is actually the workshop of a monster.

**Story**:

The documents B.J. recovered during his escape from Castle Wolfenstein contained references to "Operation Eisenfaust" -- Iron Fist. Allied intelligence flagged it as a high-priority biological weapons program. The OSA sends B.J. to investigate.

What he finds is beyond anything the Allies imagined. Dr. Schabbs, a once-brilliant surgeon stripped of his medical license before the war for "unethical experimentation," has been given unlimited resources and unlimited subjects by the SS. His mission: create soldiers that cannot die.

The mutants are real. Stitched together from the bodies of dead soldiers and prisoners, reanimated through a combination of chemical injections and experimental surgery, they shamble through the laboratory's corridors -- half-alive, partly mechanical, armed with weapons grafted directly into their flesh. They feel no pain. They follow no orders more complex than "kill."

B.J. must fight through the lab, destroy Schabbs' research, and kill the doctor himself. But the deeper he goes, the worse it gets. The early mutants are crude -- slow, clumsy. The later ones are refined. Faster. Smarter. Schabbs is learning.

**Level Progression**:
- **Levels 1-3**: The hospital facade. Seemingly normal corridors, operating rooms, wards. But the patients are wrong -- too still, too pale. Then you find the elevator down, and the real nightmare begins.
- **Levels 4-6**: The upper laboratory. Clean, clinical, bright fluorescent lights. Holding cells with failed experiments -- the things inside are still moving. The first mutant enemies appear. Chemical storage rooms, surgical theaters with blood-stained walls.
- **Levels 7-8**: The deep lab. This is where Schabbs does his real work. The walls are organic -- covered in tubes, fluids, things that pulse. The mutants here are advanced models. Multiple arms, embedded weapons, armor plating fused to their skin.
- **Levels 9-10**: Schabbs' sanctum. His personal laboratory. Specimens line the walls. His magnum opus waits.

**Boss**: Dr. Schabbs

**Tone**: Full horror. This is the darkest episode. The early levels create dread through implication -- hospital beds with restraints, surgical tools covered in rust (not rust), log books with increasingly deranged entries. The middle levels deliver on the horror with the mutant enemies. The late levels are overwhelming -- the player is surrounded by abominations, fighting through a cathedral of body horror. The Schabbs fight should feel like confronting the source of all this evil.

**Cinematic Intro**:
*An Allied reconnaissance photograph, black and white, shows a hospital complex from above. A red circle marks the location.*

*CUT TO: B.J. in a briefing room. A projector clicks through slides -- intercepted German documents, coded transmissions, a blurry photograph of a man in a surgical mask standing over something on a table that the camera mercifully cannot resolve.*

*BRIEFING OFFICER (voice-over): "Operation Eisenfaust. We don't know what it is. We know who runs it -- Dr. Theodor Schabbs, former surgeon, current monster. We know where it is. We need you to go in, find out what they're doing, and shut it down."*

*B.J. studies the photograph of Schabbs. His jaw tightens.*

*BRIEFING OFFICER: "One more thing, Blazkowicz. The last two agents we sent in... we found pieces of them."*

*B.J. stands up. He doesn't need to hear more.*

*Title card: EPISODE 2 -- OPERATION EISENFAUST*

**Cinematic Outro**:
*The laboratory burns. Explosions ripple through the underground complex as B.J. runs for the surface. Behind him, failed experiments writhe in their containment cells as fire consumes them.*

*B.J. emerges from the hospital into daylight. He is covered in blood -- some of it his, most of it not. He breathes the clean air. For a long moment, he just stands there, eyes closed.*

*Then he opens them. His face is hard. Whatever Schabbs was building, whatever Eisenfaust was meant to be, the Nazis will try again. They always do.*

*Text overlay: "Schabbs was dead. Eisenfaust was destroyed. But the war machine that created them still had a head. And B.J. knew exactly who that head belonged to."*

### Episode 3: Die, Fuhrer, Die!

**Setting**: The Fuhrerbunker -- a massive underground fortress beneath Berlin. Concrete walls twenty feet thick, blast doors, gas filtration systems, its own power plant. The most heavily defended structure in Nazi Germany. The lair of Adolf Hitler.

**Story**:

This is the mission the OSA never thought they would approve. This is the mission every Allied operative dreams of. This is the mission that, if it fails, means the death of B.J. Blazkowicz and possibly the exposure of the entire OSA network.

Kill Hitler.

Allied intelligence has confirmed that Hitler is in his Berlin bunker, personally overseeing a new weapons program. After Castle Wolfenstein, after Eisenfaust, the Allied command has decided that half-measures are not enough. They need to cut the head off the snake.

B.J. parachutes into the outskirts of Berlin under cover of a night bombing raid. He fights his way through the city's ruined streets, enters the bunker through a maintenance shaft, and descends into the most dangerous place on Earth.

The bunker is a fortress within a fortress. Elite SS bodyguards -- the Leibstandarte, Hitler's personal protection unit -- patrol every corridor. The security is layered, methodical, ruthless. And at the very bottom, in his private command center, Hitler waits.

But Hitler is not the frail, paranoid recluse the Allies expect. He has been enhanced. Augmented. The remnants of Operation Eisenfaust have been applied to the Fuhrer himself. He wears a mechanical battle suit -- a walking tank bristling with four chain guns. The madman has turned himself into a weapon.

**Level Progression**:
- **Levels 1-3**: Berlin streets and the bunker entrance. Rubble, bombed buildings, fires. The sound of Allied bombers overhead. Street fighting with Wehrmacht soldiers. Finding and entering the maintenance shaft.
- **Levels 4-6**: The outer bunker. Military command rooms, communications centers, barracks. The Leibstandarte SS are everywhere -- the best-trained, best-equipped soldiers in the Reich. Heavy resistance.
- **Levels 7-8**: The inner bunker. Hitler's personal quarters. Lavish furnishings amid concrete walls -- a madman's palace underground. Priceless stolen art on the walls, fine carpets on the floor, all surrounded by reinforced steel and guards with orders to die before retreating.
- **Levels 9-10**: The command center. The final approach. The most elite guards. And then -- the door. Behind it, the mechanical whirring of something massive.

**Boss**: Adolf Hitler (Phase 1: Mechanical Battle Suit / Phase 2: On Foot)

**Tone**: Epic. Grand. The stakes are as high as they can possibly be. The early levels have a cinematic war-movie feel -- street combat amid bombed-out Berlin, the drone of bombers, the crack of anti-aircraft guns. The bunker levels are tense and claustrophobic -- the best soldiers in the Reich versus B.J. alone. The Hitler fight is pure spectacle -- a roaring, screaming, terrifying confrontation with the embodiment of evil in a mechanical death machine.

**Cinematic Intro**:
*A map of Europe. Red arrows showing Allied advances. Blue arrows showing Nazi defenses. And at the center, Berlin -- a red dot pulsing like a heartbeat.*

*NARRATOR (B.J., voice-over, quiet and flat): "They told me this one was voluntary. That nobody would think less of me if I said no. That the odds of survival were... they didn't give me odds. They just looked at the table."*

*CUT TO: B.J. in the open door of a C-47 transport plane, night sky behind him, the distant glow of Berlin on fire below.*

*B.J. (V.O.): "I didn't say no."*

*He jumps.*

*Title card: EPISODE 3 -- DIE, FUHRER, DIE!*

**Cinematic Outro**:

*Phase 1 -- The mech suit sparks, shudders, and collapses. Hydraulic fluid and smoke pour from its joints. The chest plate cracks open, and Hitler tumbles out -- diminished without his armor, but his eyes are wild with fury. He pulls two chain guns from the wreckage.*

*Phase 2 -- After the final battle, Hitler falls. The bunker trembles as explosions cascade through the structure. B.J. stands over the body, breathing hard. He doesn't celebrate. He doesn't gloat. He just stares down at the man who caused all of this.*

*B.J. (V.O.): "It doesn't feel like I thought it would. The war isn't over. The camps aren't empty. The dead aren't coming back. But this... this is a start."*

*He turns and walks toward the exit as the bunker begins to collapse behind him.*

*Text overlay: "Hitler was dead. But the war machine he built would not die with him. And B.J.'s war was far from over."*

*SMASH CUT: The word "VICTORY" fills the screen. A brief moment of triumph before the next chapter card appears.*

### Episode 4: A Dark Secret (Prequel)

**Setting**: Castle Wolfenstein again -- but different areas. The castle's west wing, the research annex, the chemical weapons depot. Where Episode 1 was the dungeon and the eastern fortress, Episode 4 is the secret laboratories and weapons storage of the castle's other half.

**Story**:

*Three weeks before Episode 1.*

B.J.'s original mission. The OSA has received intelligence that Castle Wolfenstein houses more than just a prison -- it is the central storage facility for a new chemical weapons program run by SS-Oberfuhrer Otto Giftmacher ("Poison Maker"). The Allies need photographs, samples, and -- if possible -- Otto Giftmacher's head on a plate.

B.J. infiltrates through the castle's old drainage system. He works his way up through the underbelly of the west wing, through the chemical storage vaults, the testing chambers (where the stains on the walls tell their own story), and into Giftmacher's fortified command center.

This episode recontextualizes Episode 1. The player now understands how B.J. got captured -- not through carelessness, but because the mission went sideways at the worst possible moment. And they understand what drove B.J.'s silent fury during his imprisonment: he had seen what was being made in those labs.

**Level Progression**:
- **Levels 1-3**: The drainage system and lower west wing. Ancient tunnels, flooded passages, the castle's medieval bones. B.J. enters stealthily -- the tone is espionage, infiltration. Guards are sparse but alert. One alarm and the mission is over.
- **Levels 4-6**: The chemical storage vaults. Sealed rooms, hazmat warnings, barrels of agent marked with skull symbols. Gas-masked soldiers patrol. Some rooms have a greenish haze -- B.J. must move quickly through them or take damage.
- **Levels 7-8**: The testing chambers. This is where Giftmacher tests his weapons. The evidence of what happened here is everywhere. Restraint chairs. Observation windows. Log books written in clinical, detached language about "subjects" and "exposure times." The guards here are Giftmacher's personal team -- fanatically loyal.
- **Levels 9-10**: Giftmacher's command center. Fortified, heavily guarded, and rigged with traps. Otto Giftmacher waits behind blast doors.

**Boss**: Otto Giftmacher

**Tone**: Espionage thriller turning into chemical horror. The early levels feel like a spy movie -- infiltration, stealth, careful movement. The chemical storage levels introduce a sickening dread -- the player knows what these substances are for. The testing chambers are gut-wrenching. The boss fight is aggressive and desperate -- Giftmacher fights dirty, using his own chemical weapons.

**Cinematic Intro**:
*Text: "THREE WEEKS EARLIER"*

*Night. Rain. The silhouette of Castle Wolfenstein against a lightning-cracked sky. A small inflatable raft crosses the castle's moat. One man. One mission.*

*B.J. (V.O.): "Castle Wolfenstein. The OSA briefing said it was a prison. That was a lie. Not deliberately -- they just didn't know. Nobody knew what was really inside those walls. I was about to find out."*

*B.J. pulls himself through a drainage grate. The camera follows him into darkness.*

*Title card: EPISODE 4 -- A DARK SECRET*

**Cinematic Outro**:
*B.J. fights his way to the west wing exit, photographs and chemical samples secured. He reaches a window, looks out -- freedom is fifty meters away. The tree line, the darkness, escape.*

*Then the floodlights hit him. A voice on a loudspeaker -- Hans Grosse's voice, cold and amused: "Herr Blazkowicz. We have been expecting you."*

*B.J. turns. Fifty SS soldiers, weapons raised. Behind them, Hans Grosse, arms folded, smiling.*

*B.J. (V.O.): "That's how they got me. Not because I failed. Because someone sold me out. And in the two weeks that followed, chained in that cell, I had plenty of time to think about what I'd seen in those labs. Plenty of time to plan what I'd do when I got out."*

*SMASH CUT TO: The opening shot of Episode 1 -- B.J. in the cell. Full circle.*

*Text overlay: "B.J. was captured. But what he saw in Giftmacher's labs would haunt him -- and fuel the rage that would carry him through everything that followed."*

### Episode 5: Trail of the Madman

**Setting**: Castle Erlangen, a fortress in the Thuringian Forest. Where Castle Wolfenstein was ancient and cold, Erlangen is a modernized military fortress -- concrete reinforcements over medieval stone, radar installations on the towers, a motor pool in the courtyard. It is the command center of the Grosse family's SS division.

**Story**:

Hans Grosse is dead. B.J. killed him in Castle Wolfenstein. But the Grosse bloodline runs deep in the SS, and Hans had a sister -- Obersturmbannfuhrer Gretel Grosse. Where Hans was a brute -- massive, loud, direct -- Gretel is something worse. She is a tactician. A hunter. And she has made it personal.

Since her brother's death, Gretel has been hunting B.J. She has studied his methods, his patterns, his known associates. She has killed three OSA informants in the process. The Allied intelligence network in southern Germany is collapsing. Gretel is dismantling it, one agent at a time, and she is getting closer to the OSA itself.

The OSA decides to strike first. B.J. is sent to Castle Erlangen to end the Grosse line permanently.

But Gretel knows he is coming. She has been waiting for this. The castle is a trap -- and B.J. is walking into it with his eyes open.

**Level Progression**:
- **Levels 1-3**: The forest approach and outer defenses. Minefields, patrol dogs, sniper towers. The castle looms ahead. Gretel's voice crackles over captured radio frequencies: "Welcome, Blazkowicz. My brother spoke of you before you killed him. I have been looking forward to this."
- **Levels 4-6**: The modernized fortress. Concrete corridors, armored doors, security checkpoints. Gretel's elite guards -- hand-picked, fanatically loyal, equipped with the best weapons the Reich can provide. The castle is designed to channel intruders into kill zones.
- **Levels 7-8**: The command wing. Map rooms, communications centers, Gretel's personal quarters. Evidence of her obsession with B.J. -- photographs, dossiers, mission reports, a wall covered in string connecting locations and names. She has been building a profile.
- **Levels 9-10**: The inner sanctum. Gretel's arena. She has prepared this space for B.J. specifically. It is a killing floor, designed to her advantage.

**Boss**: Gretel Grosse

**Tone**: Predator vs. predator. This episode is a duel -- two elite warriors circling each other. The tension comes not from horror but from respect. B.J. knows Gretel is dangerous. Gretel knows B.J. is dangerous. The entire castle is a chess match, and every level is a move. The radio taunts from Gretel give this episode a personal edge that the others lack. This is not ideology. This is vendetta.

**Cinematic Intro**:
*A dossier stamped "CLASSIFIED" opens on screen. Photographs of Hans Grosse -- alive, then dead. Next page: a woman's photograph. Similar jaw, same cold eyes, but sharper. More focused.*

*BRIEFING OFFICER (V.O.): "Gretel Grosse. Hans's sister. SS-Obersturmbannfuhrer. Commander of the 14th SS Panzer-Jaeger Division. Three of our people are dead because of her, Blazkowicz. She's not just cleaning up after her brother -- she's hunting us."*

*B.J. studies the photograph.*

*BRIEFING OFFICER: "She knows you're coming. In fact, we think she wants you to come."*

*B.J. sets the photograph down.*

*B.J.: "Then let's not disappoint her."*

*Title card: EPISODE 5 -- TRAIL OF THE MADMAN*

**Cinematic Outro**:
*Gretel falls. Unlike the other bosses, she does not scream. She does not beg. She drops to one knee, then looks up at B.J. with something that might be respect.*

*GRETEL (dying): "Better than Hans... better than me... but not better than what comes next, Blazkowicz. Fettgesicht will... finish what we started..."*

*She collapses. B.J. stands over her for a moment. There is no satisfaction in his face. Just exhaustion.*

*B.J. (V.O.): "She was the best soldier they had. The most dangerous person I ever faced. And with her last breath, she warned me. Fettgesicht. The Fat Face. The last name on the list."*

*Text overlay: "The Grosse line was ended. But Gretel's warning pointed to one final enemy -- the supreme commander of the Nazi war machine's darkest operations. The end was near."*

### Episode 6: Confrontation

**Setting**: Fortress Hollehammer -- the final Nazi stronghold. Built into the side of a mountain, it is a self-contained military city. Barracks, factories, laboratories, command centers -- everything the Nazi war machine needs to survive even if Berlin falls. This is the doomsday fortress, and it is commanded by General Fettgesicht, the architect of every horror B.J. has faced.

**Story**:

Fettgesicht -- "Fat Face" -- is not a field commander. He is the man behind the curtain. He approved Giftmacher's chemical weapons program. He funded Schabbs' mutation experiments. He armed Hans Grosse. He mentored Gretel. Every nightmare B.J. has faced traces back to this man.

And now Fettgesicht is preparing his masterstroke: a final, massive deployment of Schabbs' mutant technology combined with Giftmacher's chemical weapons. A doomsday weapon. If it launches, the tide of the war turns back in Germany's favor.

The Allies cannot let this happen. B.J. must infiltrate Hollehammer, stop the weapon, and kill Fettgesicht. This is the last mission. The final confrontation. Everything has led to this.

**Level Progression**:
- **Levels 1-3**: The mountain approach and outer fortress. Carved into rock, gun emplacements, heavy fortifications. The most heavily defended position B.J. has ever seen. Every type of enemy -- guards, officers, SS, mutants, dogs -- all present, all concentrated.
- **Levels 4-6**: The industrial complex. Weapons factories, ammunition stores, vehicle bays. The scale is staggering. B.J. is one man against an army. But the labyrinthine factory layout provides cover and opportunities.
- **Levels 7-8**: The research wing. Here, Schabbs' work continues under new scientists. Advanced mutants, chemical weapons in production, the doomsday weapon taking shape. B.J. plants explosives (environmental interaction moments).
- **Levels 9-10**: Fettgesicht's command center. Deep in the mountain's heart. The general waits, surrounded by his personal guard. The approach is a gauntlet. The fight is everything.

**Boss**: General Fettgesicht

**Tone**: Apocalyptic finale. This is the biggest, loudest, most relentless episode. Every type of enemy. Every weapon. No breathing room. The industrial levels are a spectacle of scale. The research wing combines the horror of Episode 2 with the combat intensity of Episode 3. And the final boss fight is designed to be the hardest, most epic confrontation in the game -- a fitting end to B.J.'s war.

**Cinematic Intro**:
*Aerial footage of a mountain range. Snow-capped peaks, pristine forest. Beautiful, peaceful. Then the camera descends, and the fortress reveals itself -- gun turrets emerging from the rock face, armored doors in the mountainside, smoke from underground factories.*

*B.J. (V.O.): "Hollehammer. The mountain that swallowed an army. I've killed their jailer, their scientist, their Fuhrer, their poisoner, and their hunter. One name left. Fettgesicht. The man who made them all."*

*BRIEFING OFFICER (V.O.): "Intelligence says the fortress garrison is over two thousand men. Plus an unknown number of... Schabbs' legacy."*

*Silence.*

*B.J. (V.O.): "Two thousand and one, then."*

*He chambers a round.*

*Title card: EPISODE 6 -- CONFRONTATION*

**Cinematic Outro (Game Ending)**:
*Fettgesicht falls. The mountain shakes. The explosives B.J. planted in the research wing detonate in sequence -- a cascading chain of destruction that rips through the fortress. Hollehammer is dying.*

*B.J. runs. Through collapsing corridors, past crumbling walls, over the bodies of his enemies. Fire and dust pursue him. He reaches the outer fortification as the mountain's face cracks open behind him. He throws himself through the blast door just as it seals.*

*Outside. Dawn. The mountain is on fire, smoke pouring from every opening. The fortress -- the last fortress -- crumbles inward, swallowing itself.*

*B.J. walks away. Down the mountain path, through the forest, into the morning light. His weapons are empty. His uniform is shredded. He is bleeding from a dozen wounds.*

*But he is alive. And they are not.*

*B.J. (V.O.): "It's over. Not the war -- that'll take the rest of us a while longer. But this part. The castles, the labs, the bunkers, the monsters. The names on the list. They're all crossed off now."*

*He reaches a ridge overlooking a valley. In the distance, a column of Allied tanks moves along a road. The war continues. The world turns.*

*B.J. (V.O.): "Someone once asked me what I fight for. I didn't have an answer then. I do now. I fight for the day I don't have to fight anymore."*

*He sits down on the ridge, watches the sunrise, and for the first time in the game -- for the first time in a long time -- he closes his eyes. Not in pain. In peace.*

*FADE TO BLACK.*

*Title card: WOLFENSTEIN 3D*
*"The war was won. Not by armies alone, but by the men and women who walked into the darkness so others wouldn't have to."*

*Roll credits.*

## 2. Character Profiles

### William "B.J." Blazkowicz -- Player Character

**Full Name**: William Joseph Blazkowicz
**Rank**: Captain, United States Army / OSA Field Operative, Classification: Ultra
**Age**: 31
**Nationality**: American (Polish descent)

**Physical Description**:
- Height: 6'2" (188 cm)
- Build: Heavy muscular, 220 lbs. Working-class strong, not gym-sculpted
- Hair: Dark brown, cropped military short, perpetually disheveled
- Eyes: Steel blue, deep-set, with permanent dark circles
- Face: Square jaw, prominent brow, twice-broken nose. Handsome in a battered, weathered way
- Distinguishing marks: Scar across left cheek (Episode 4 origin). Burn scar on right forearm. Dog tags always visible
- Clothing: Standard Army officer's uniform, increasingly torn and blood-stained as episodes progress

**Personality**:
B.J. is a man of few words and enormous action. He is not stoic by nature -- he is stoic by necessity. He feels deeply: rage at what the Nazis do, grief for the people he cannot save, determination that borders on obsession. But he compartmentalizes with the efficiency of a man who knows that feeling too much in the field will get him killed.

He has a dry, grim sense of humor that emerges at unexpected moments -- usually when things are at their worst. It is his coping mechanism. He is not cruel, not sadistic. He takes no pleasure in killing. But he does not hesitate, either. He is a weapon with a conscience.

**Motivation**: Not patriotism in the abstract. B.J. fights because he has seen the camps, the labs, the experiments. He fights for the specific, concrete people the Nazis have destroyed. Every mission is personal.

**Fighting Style**: Aggressive, adaptive, relentless. B.J. uses whatever is available -- fists, knife, pistol, machine gun, chain gun. He prefers overwhelming firepower but can operate with stealth when necessary. He has an almost supernatural ability to absorb punishment and keep fighting.

**Key Dialogue Lines**:
- (Episode 1, finding a weapon) "One gun. That's all I've ever needed."
- (Episode 2, discovering the mutants) "...God. What have they done to these people?"
- (Episode 3, entering the bunker) "All roads lead here."
- (Episode 4, seeing chemical weapons) "This is what they're building. This is what the war is really about."
- (Episode 5, responding to Gretel's taunts) "Keep talking. Makes you easier to find."
- (Episode 6, final level) "Last one."
- (Finding a secret room) "Hm. Sloppy."
- (Picking up health at low HP) "Not done yet."
- (Facing a boss) "Just you and me."
- (After a boss fight) *Silence. Heavy breathing. Then walks forward.*

### Hans Grosse -- Episode 1 Boss

**Full Name**: Hans Friedrich Grosse
**Rank**: SS-Obersturmbannfuhrer (Lieutenant Colonel)
**Age**: 44
**Role**: Warden of Castle Wolfenstein

**Physical Description**:
- Height: 6'8" (203 cm)
- Build: Massive. 310 lbs of muscle and menace. Barrel chest, tree-trunk arms
- Hair: Shaved bald
- Eyes: Small, dark, piggish -- cruel intelligence behind a brutish exterior
- Face: Wide, flat, with a permanent scowl. Deep scar running from forehead to chin (left side). Missing left ear (replaced with a metal prosthetic)
- Armor: Wears a modified flak vest reinforced with steel plates. Heavy boots with steel toes
- Weapons: Dual chain guns, custom-built for his size. He carries them like pistols

**Personality**:
Hans is a sadist who has found the perfect job. As warden of Castle Wolfenstein, he has absolute authority over life and death. He takes his time with prisoners -- not for intelligence (he cares nothing for information) but for entertainment. He is crude, loud, and terrifyingly direct. When he enters a room, the air changes. Even his own officers fear him.

But Hans is not stupid. He is cunning in the way of a predator -- patient when it suits him, explosive when it doesn't. He has held his position for six years because he is very, very good at keeping people locked up. And at making sure they never want to try escaping.

**Motivation**: Power. Control. The pleasure of dominance. Hans is not ideologically driven -- he would be a monster in any uniform. The Nazi party simply gave him permission.

**Fighting Style**: Overwhelming force. Hans doesn't dodge, doesn't take cover, doesn't retreat. He walks forward, chain guns blazing, and dares you to stand in front of him. His sheer size and armor make him a walking tank.

**Key Dialogue Lines**:
- (Boss intro) "BLAZKOWICZ! You thought you could leave my castle? NOBODY LEAVES MY CASTLE!"
- (During fight, player hiding) "Come out, little man! Hans wants to play!"
- (Taking damage) "Ha! That tickled!"
- (Below 50% health) "You... you will pay for every drop of my blood. EVERY DROP!"

**Death Scene**:
Hans staggers, chain guns dropping from his hands. He looks down at his wounds with genuine surprise -- as if he truly believed he was invincible. He drops to his knees, the floor cracking under his weight. He looks up at B.J., and for one moment, the cruelty leaves his eyes, replaced by something almost like respect.

HANS: "Not... bad... for a little man..."

He falls forward. The impact shakes the room. Dust rains from the ceiling. The warden of Castle Wolfenstein is dead.

### Dr. Theodor Schabbs -- Episode 2 Boss

**Full Name**: Dr. Theodor Ernst Schabbs
**Rank**: SS-Brigadefuhrer (honorary), Chief Scientist of Operation Eisenfaust
**Age**: 58
**Role**: Creator of the Mutant soldier program

**Physical Description**:
- Height: 5'6" (168 cm)
- Build: Soft, pudgy, physically unimposing. The least threatening-looking person in the game
- Hair: Wild grey-white, Einstein-esque but unwashed, stained with unknown substances
- Eyes: Watery blue, magnified by thick round spectacles. Constantly darting, assessing, measuring
- Face: Doughy, pale from years underground. Thin-lipped smile that never quite reaches his eyes. Clean-shaven but always with a five o'clock shadow
- Clothing: Stained surgical gown over an SS uniform. Rubber gloves that he never removes. A surgical mask hanging around his neck
- Weapons: Syringes filled with mutagen compound (thrown as projectiles). Also carries a Luger

**Personality**:
Schabbs is the most disturbing character in the game because he is calm. While Hans rages and Gretel schemes, Schabbs simply... works. He views his mutant experiments with the detachment of a mechanic tuning an engine. His subjects are not people -- they are components. Materials. His journal entries (found throughout Episode 2) are written in clinical, academic language that makes the horrors they describe even worse.

He was a respected surgeon before the war. He was stripped of his license for performing unauthorized experiments on patients -- testing the limits of human pain tolerance, exploring how much of a body could be replaced or removed while maintaining consciousness. The SS didn't punish him. They hired him.

**Motivation**: Pure scientific curiosity, stripped of all ethical constraints. Schabbs does not care about the war, about the Reich, about ideology. He cares about answering questions: Can the dead be reanimated? Can a human body be improved? Can mortality be overcome? The Nazis give him unlimited subjects and unlimited funding. That is all he needs.

**Fighting Style**: Indirect. Schabbs himself is physically weak -- he relies on his creations to do the fighting. During the boss encounter, he stays at range, throwing syringes that deal damage and temporarily distort the player's vision (the mutagen affects living tissue too). He is surprisingly fast and evasive for his age and build -- adrenaline and madness make him nimble.

**Key Dialogue Lines**:
- (Boss intro) *Adjusting his spectacles, looking at B.J. like a specimen.* "Ah. Blazkowicz. I've read your file. Remarkable constitution. You would make... an excellent subject."
- (During fight) "Hold still! This will only hurt for a moment -- well, perhaps longer!"
- (Summoning mutants) "Children! Protect your father!"
- (Taking damage) "Pain is just... data... JUST DATA!"
- (Below 50% health) "No! My work! You cannot destroy my work! It's too IMPORTANT!"

**Death Scene**:
A syringe shatters. Schabbs clutches his chest, stumbling backward into one of his own operating tables. Equipment crashes around him. He slides to the floor, spectacles cracked, and looks up at B.J. with an expression of offended disbelief -- not fear, but indignation.

SCHABBS: "You fool... you understand nothing... I was going to conquer death itself... I was going to make us... immortal..."

His voice trails off. His eyes go glassy. His hand reaches feebly toward a syringe on the floor -- one last experiment, one last attempt -- and falls short. Dr. Schabbs dies surrounded by his life's work, and none of it can save him.

### Adolf Hitler -- Episode 3 Boss

**Rank**: Fuhrer of the Third Reich
**Age**: 54 (in 1943)
**Role**: Supreme leader. Final boss of the original trilogy.

**Physical Description (Phase 1 -- Mech Suit)**:
- The Panzerfuhrer Battle Suit: A 9-foot-tall bipedal mechanical exoskeleton, painted gunmetal grey with red and black Nazi insignia
- Four mounted MG42 chain guns (two per arm)
- Armored cockpit housing Hitler, visible through a reinforced glass viewport
- Hydraulic legs, each footstep shaking the floor
- Smoke vents, sparking wires, the hiss of pneumatics -- raw, brutal, Frankenstein technology

**Physical Description (Phase 2 -- On Foot)**:
- Height: 5'9" (175 cm)
- Build: Thin, wiry, but manic energy makes him seem larger
- The iconic mustache, now singed and askew
- Wild eyes, pupils dilated, veins visible at the temples
- Uniform torn and burned from the mech suit's destruction
- Dual chain guns, one in each hand, firing wildly
- Movements are erratic -- fueled by rage, drugs, and desperation

**Personality**:
In this version, Hitler is not portrayed as a complex character -- he is the final monster. The game does not humanize him or attempt to explore his psychology. He is the endpoint of evil, the reason all of this exists. His battle suit represents everything wrong with the Nazi ideology: the belief that technology and willpower can overcome any obstacle, that one man's vision can dominate the world.

In Phase 1, he is the machine -- cold, powerful, inhuman. In Phase 2, stripped of his armor, he is the man underneath: small, pathetic, but still dangerous. Still lethal. The transition between phases is the thematic core of the fight: strip away the machinery of power, and what remains is still a killer.

**Motivation**: Absolute power. Survival. In this moment, cornered in his bunker with B.J. at the door, Hitler fights not for ideology but for his own life.

**Fighting Style**:
- Phase 1: Methodical destruction. The mech suit moves slowly but covers the arena with overlapping fields of fire from four chain guns. Rocket pods fire in patterns. The player must use pillars and cover while finding openings to damage the suit's weak points (hydraulic joints, coolant vents).
- Phase 2: Berserker. Hitler on foot is fast, erratic, and unpredictable. He charges, fires wildly, screams incoherently. Less dangerous than the mech suit but harder to hit.

**Key Dialogue Lines**:
- (Phase 1 intro, mech suit powering up) "You dare? YOU DARE come into MY domain? I am the FUTURE of this world! I am UNSTOPPABLE!"
- (During Phase 1) "BURN! BURN IN THE FIRES OF THE REICH!"
- (Phase 1 to Phase 2 transition, mech suit crumbling) "No... NO! The suit -- it doesn't matter! I DON'T NEED IT! I'LL KILL YOU WITH MY BARE HANDS!"
- (Phase 2, erratic) "I am Adolf Hitler! I CANNOT DIE! HISTORY WILL NOT ALLOW IT!"
- (Below 25% health) "Nein... nein... NEIN!"

**Death Scene**:
Hitler drops his guns. He backs into the wall, hands raised -- not in surrender, but in disbelief. His mouth opens and closes. For one moment, he is just a small, wounded man in a bunker, facing the consequence of everything he has done.

No final words. No grand speech. He crumples. Falls. Lies still.

The silence after is the most powerful moment in the game. No music. No sound effects. Just B.J.'s breathing and the distant rumble of the bunker settling.

Then B.J. turns and walks away. He doesn't look back.

### Otto Giftmacher -- Episode 4 Boss

**Full Name**: Otto Werner Giftmacher
**Rank**: SS-Oberfuhrer (Senior Colonel)
**Age**: 51
**Role**: Head of the Chemical Weapons Division

**Physical Description**:
- Height: 5'11" (180 cm)
- Build: Lean, angular, with the precision of a man who measures everything
- Hair: Thinning, combed meticulously. Steel grey
- Eyes: Green, cold, analytical. Never blink when they should
- Face: Gaunt, hollow-cheeked, hawkish nose. A face built for looking down at people
- Chemical burns on both hands (old, scarred over -- occupational hazard)
- Clothing: Immaculate SS uniform under a heavy-duty chemical protection apron. Gas mask hanging at his belt, always within reach
- Weapons: Modified flamethrower that disperses chemical agents in a wide arc. Also carries chemical grenades

**Personality**:
Where Schabbs is chaotic genius, Giftmacher is ordered evil. He is meticulous, precise, and deeply invested in the aesthetics of his work. He keeps his laboratory spotless. His notes are calligraphied. His chemical formulas are written with the care of a poet composing verse. He believes chemical weapons are an art form -- elegant, efficient, beautiful in their mathematics.

He is also a true believer. Unlike Hans (who is a thug) or Schabbs (who is amoral), Giftmacher genuinely believes in the Nazi cause. He sees his weapons as tools of destiny -- the means by which the "superior race" will secure its future. This makes him more dangerous than the others because he will never stop, never doubt, never question.

**Motivation**: Ideological purity expressed through chemistry. Every formula is a prayer. Every weapon is a sacrament.

**Fighting Style**: Area denial. Giftmacher floods zones with chemical clouds that damage over time. He forces the player to keep moving, never staying in one place. His personal weapon creates expanding clouds of agent that linger. He retreats methodically, leading the player through pre-prepared kill zones. Fighting him is like fighting a chess player who set up the board before you arrived.

**Key Dialogue Lines**:
- (Boss intro, standing behind a wall of green gas) "Blazkowicz. Do you know what nerve agent does to the human body? Allow me to demonstrate."
- (During fight, deploying chemicals) "Breathe deeply. My finest blend."
- (Player avoiding gas clouds) "Impressive reflexes. But you cannot hold your breath forever."
- (Taking damage) "Interesting. Most subjects stop fighting by now."
- (Below 50% health) "The formula is PERFECT. YOU are the flaw!"

**Death Scene**:
A chemical canister on Giftmacher's belt ruptures from B.J.'s gunfire. Green gas hisses from the crack. Giftmacher looks down at it with the first genuine emotion the player has seen from him: terror.

GIFTMACHER: "No... not like this... not by my own..."

He fumbles for his gas mask. His hands -- the scarred, burned hands of a man who has handled poison his entire life -- shake too badly to secure the seal. The gas envelops him. He falls to his knees, choking.

GIFTMACHER (whispering): "...beautiful... formula..."

He collapses into his own cloud. Poetic justice -- the poisoner, poisoned.

B.J. watches through the observation window, gas mask secured. He says nothing. There is nothing to say.

### Gretel Grosse -- Episode 5 Boss

**Full Name**: Gretel Annelise Grosse
**Rank**: SS-Obersturmbannfuhrer (Lieutenant Colonel)
**Age**: 38
**Role**: Commander of the 14th SS Panzer-Jaeger Division, Hans Grosse's sister

**Physical Description**:
- Height: 6'1" (185 cm)
- Build: Tall, athletic, powerfully built but lean -- a predator's physique. 190 lbs of coiled muscle
- Hair: Platinum blonde, pulled back in a severe military bun
- Eyes: Ice blue, identical to her brother's but far more intelligent. Predatory focus
- Face: Angular, high cheekbones, strong jaw (the family resemblance to Hans is clear but refined). A thin scar across her right eyebrow
- Distinguishing features: Moves with eerie, controlled grace -- every step deliberate, every gesture efficient
- Clothing: Custom-fitted SS officer's uniform, impeccably tailored. Black leather gloves. Knight's Cross at her throat
- Weapons: Dual chain guns (family tradition), but she also carries a combat knife and is proficient at close range

**Personality**:
Gretel is the most dangerous enemy B.J. faces -- not because she is the strongest, but because she is the smartest. Where Hans was a battering ram, Gretel is a scalpel. She studied military strategy at university before joining the SS. She has commanded armored divisions in combat and earned the Knight's Cross for tactical brilliance, not just bravery.

She loved her brother. Not in a sentimental way -- in the way that one predator respects another. His death at B.J.'s hands did not make her grief-stricken. It made her curious. What kind of man could kill Hans Grosse? She has spent months building a profile of B.J., studying his methods, anticipating his movements. She knows him better than anyone alive.

There is a mutual respect between Gretel and B.J. that neither would admit. They are mirrors -- elite warriors on opposite sides of an unbridgeable divide. In another world, they might have understood each other.

**Motivation**: Honor the Grosse name. Prove herself superior to the man who killed her brother. And yes, serve the Reich -- but her loyalty is to family first, ideology second.

**Fighting Style**: Tactical aggression. Gretel uses the environment, retreating and advancing strategically, drawing B.J. into ambush positions. She taunts via radio to provoke mistakes. In direct combat, she combines her chain guns' firepower with unexpected close-range rushes -- knife slashes that interrupt the player's rhythm. She is the only boss who actively tries to outthink the player.

**Key Dialogue Lines**:
- (Radio taunt, Level 1) "Welcome to Castle Erlangen, Blazkowicz. I've been preparing for you. I hope you appreciate the effort."
- (Radio taunt, Level 4) "My brother was strong. But strong is not enough, is it? You taught us that."
- (Radio taunt, Level 7) "I've read your file. Milwaukee boy. Polish grandmother. She would be proud of you. Or horrified. Hard to say."
- (Boss intro, stepping out of shadows) "No more radio games, Blazkowicz. Just us now."
- (During fight, after a close exchange) "Good. GOOD! Show me what you showed Hans!"
- (Taking damage) *Sharp breath through teeth.* "Better than I expected."
- (Below 50% health) "You... are everything they said you were. But I am a GROSSE. We do not fall easily."

**Death Scene**:
Gretel drops to one knee, chain guns clattering to the floor. She does not clutch her wounds. She does not show pain. She looks up at B.J. with those ice-blue eyes, and for a moment, there is no hatred in them. Only recognition.

GRETEL: "Better than Hans. Better than me." *A pause.* "Fettgesicht will not be so... sporting. He has no honor, Blazkowicz. Not like us."

She straightens her uniform with bloodied hands. Pulls the Knight's Cross from her throat. Holds it for a moment. Then lets it fall.

GRETEL: "The Grosse name... dies with me. Make it worth something, Blazkowicz. End this war."

She closes her eyes. Falls. Silence.

B.J. stands there for a long moment. Then, almost imperceptibly, he nods.

It is the most respect he has ever shown an enemy.

### General Fettgesicht -- Episode 6 Boss

**Full Name**: General Wilhelm Karl Fettgesicht
**Rank**: SS-Obergruppenführer (General)
**Age**: 62
**Role**: Supreme commander of all secret weapons programs. The architect behind everything.

**Physical Description**:
- Height: 5'10" (178 cm)
- Build: Heavy, corpulent. The name "Fettgesicht" (Fat Face) is accurate -- he is a large, physically imposing man, but his mass is not muscle. He is soft, indulgent, and carries himself with the entitled bearing of old Prussian aristocracy
- Hair: White, thinning, slicked back
- Eyes: Small, deep-set, calculating. Permanently narrowed, as if everything he sees is being evaluated for usefulness
- Face: Round, fleshy, with deep jowls and a permanent flush. A small, cruel mouth. Monocle over the right eye
- Distinguishing features: Gold rings on every finger. A dueling scar on his left cheek -- souvenir of his Heidelberg university days. Walks with a slight limp (old war wound he never lets anyone forget)
- Clothing: Heavily decorated SS general's uniform, dripping with medals and commendations. Leather greatcoat. Riding boots, polished to a mirror shine
- Weapons: A custom-built rocket launcher combined with a heavy machine gun -- a weapon as excessive as the man himself. He also wears a powered exoskeleton frame beneath his greatcoat that enhances his strength and absorbs damage

**Personality**:
Fettgesicht is the most purely evil character in the game because he is the most rational. He is not a sadist like Hans, not a madman like Schabbs, not a fanatic like Giftmacher. He is a bureaucrat of atrocity. He approved every program, signed every funding request, reviewed every progress report, and never once lost sleep. He is evil by calculation, not passion.

He comes from Prussian military aristocracy -- old money, old power, old cruelty refined over generations. He joined the Nazi party not out of ideological fervor but because it was the path to power. He would have been equally comfortable commanding any army that gave him absolute authority.

He has never killed a man with his own hands. He has never visited Schabbs' laboratory, never toured Giftmacher's testing chambers. He has only read the reports, approved the budgets, and moved to the next item on his agenda. He is the man who makes monsters possible.

**Motivation**: Power, legacy, survival. Fettgesicht does not care about ideology. He cares about winning. If the Allies win, he hangs. If Germany wins, he rules. It is that simple.

**Fighting Style**: Overwhelming firepower backed by technological superiority. His exoskeleton makes him faster and more durable than his appearance suggests. His weapon deals massive damage at all ranges. He fights from elevated positions, uses the arena's terrain, and deploys environmental hazards (gas vents, electrified floors, collapsing pillars). He is the hardest boss not because he is the most skilled but because he has spent decades preparing for exactly this scenario.

**Key Dialogue Lines**:
- (Boss intro, seated behind his desk, not even standing when B.J. enters) "Blazkowicz. Do sit down. We should talk."
- (Standing, revealing his weapon) "No? Pity. I had hoped this would be civilized." *Smiles thinly.* "Grosse warned you about me, didn't she? Good girl. She was always my best student."
- (During fight) "You have cost me a great deal, Blazkowicz. Giftmacher. Schabbs. The Grosses. Years of investment, destroyed by one stubborn American."
- (Taking damage) "Impressive. But I have spent sixty years preparing for men like you."
- (Below 50% health, dropping composed facade) "No -- NO! I will NOT be undone by a GRUNT! I am the architect! I am the VISION!"
- (Below 25% health, desperate) "Guards! GUARDS! Someone -- ANYONE!"
- (No guards come. Silence.) "...I see."

**Death Scene**:
The exoskeleton sputters and dies. Fettgesicht's weapon falls from hands that can no longer hold it. He stumbles backward into his desk chair, collapsing into it as if it were a throne.

For a long moment, he simply sits there, breathing heavily, staring at B.J. with those calculating eyes. Even now, he is assessing. Even now, some part of his mind is looking for an angle, an escape, a deal to be made.

FETTGESICHT: "You... you think this changes anything? Kill me, and another takes my place. The machine... the machine does not need me. It does not need anyone. It simply... continues."

B.J. says nothing. He raises his weapon.

FETTGESICHT (quietly, the composure cracking): "I am... I was going to win this war. I had PLANS. Strategies. The doomsday weapon would have--"

B.J. fires.

Fettgesicht jerks once. The monocle falls. He slumps.

The machine stops.

B.J. turns to leave. Behind him, on Fettgesicht's desk, a folder stamped "DOOMSDAY" sits open. B.J. pauses. Picks it up. Reads. His jaw tightens. He tucks it into his jacket.

Evidence. For the trials that will come.

### Episode Intro Format (Template)

Each episode intro follows this structure:
1. **Establishing shot** (5 seconds): Location reveal, setting the visual tone
2. **Context narration** (10-15 seconds): B.J. voice-over or briefing officer exposition
3. **Personal stakes** (5-10 seconds): Why this mission matters to B.J. specifically
4. **Action beat** (5 seconds): B.J. preparing, loading weapons, entering the location
5. **Title card** (3 seconds): Episode title with dramatic musical sting

**Total duration**: 30-45 seconds per intro
**All intros are skippable** but designed to reward watching

## 4. Environmental Storytelling

### General Principles

The levels tell stories through their environments. The player is never required to notice these elements, but attentive players are rewarded with a richer, more horrifying picture of what the Nazis have built.

**Visual Storytelling Rules**:
- Show, don't tell. A blood trail leading to a locked door says more than any text
- Contrast is key: beautiful medieval architecture defiled by Nazi occupation
- Repetition with variation: propaganda posters that change subtly between levels
- Environmental progression: early levels are relatively "clean"; later levels show the true horror

---

### Episode 1: Castle Wolfenstein

**Propaganda Posters**:
- "VICTORY THROUGH STRENGTH" -- standard Wehrmacht recruitment
- "THE WALLS HAVE EARS" -- security warnings (ironic, given B.J.'s escape)
- Defaced posters in the dungeon -- prisoners have scratched messages: "GOD HELP US," "NO ESCAPE," and one in Polish that reads "THEY WILL PAY"

**Environmental Details**:
- **Dungeon cells**: Each cell tells a story. One has scratch marks counting days (312 marks). One has a crude map scratched into the wall. One is empty except for dark stains.
- **Guard quarters**: Personal effects humanize the enemy -- family photographs, letters, a half-finished chess game. These soldiers are people. That makes what they're guarding worse.
- **Hans Grosse's office**: A wall of confiscated items -- watches, rings, wallets. Trophies from prisoners. A crystal decanter of schnapps, half-empty. An interrogation schedule pinned to the wall with B.J.'s name on it, tomorrow's date circled.

**Secret Rooms**:
- A hidden chamber behind a loose wall stone contains an OSA dead drop -- documents and a medical kit, left by the agent before B.J. The documents are bloodstained. The agent didn't make it.
- A walled-up section of the dungeon contains medieval torture equipment. The Nazis found it and put it back into service. Tool marks show recent use.

**Audio Cues**:
- Distant screams (other prisoners, never seen)
- Guard dogs barking in the kennels
- The castle creaking and groaning -- it is ancient and alive
- Hans Grosse's laughter echoing through corridors (before the boss fight)

---

### Episode 2: Operation Eisenfaust

**Environmental Details**:
- **Hospital facade**: Normal-looking corridors with an uncanny wrongness. Beds are made too perfectly. No patients are visible. The flowers on the reception desk are plastic. Everything is staged.
- **Laboratory entrance**: A sign reads "ZUGANG VERBOTEN" (Access Forbidden). Behind it, the temperature drops. The lighting changes from warm hospital yellow to cold fluorescent blue.
- **Holding cells**: Glass-walled observation rooms containing failed experiments. Some are still moving -- twitching, reaching toward the glass. The player cannot interact with them. They are beyond help.
- **Schabbs' notes**: Found on clipboards throughout the lab. Written in neat, academic handwriting:
  - "Subject 47: Locomotor function restored. Cognitive function nil. Acceptable."
  - "Subject 112: Rejected grafts. Disposal recommended."
  - "Subject 238: Full motor control. Responds to basic commands. Promising. Schedule for weapons integration."
- **The operating theaters**: Stainless steel tables with restraints. Drains in the floor. Surgical tools laid out with precision. Some tools have no medical equivalent -- they were designed for Schabbs' specific purposes.
- **The deep lab walls**: Organic-looking growth -- tubes, nutrient systems, things that pulse. The laboratory has become a living thing. Schabbs' experiments have outgrown their containers.

**Secret Rooms**:
- A sealed observation room overlooking Schabbs' main lab. Inside: a journal from an assistant scientist who grew horrified by the experiments and tried to document them for the Allies. The last entry: "He knows. God forgive me."
- A room containing Schabbs' "first success" -- a preserved, immobile mutant in a glass case, labeled "ALPHA." It looks almost human. Almost.

**Audio Cues**:
- The mutant moan -- constant, ambient, growing louder as the player descends deeper
- Dripping fluids (not water)
- Machinery hum -- the lab never sleeps
- Distant, rhythmic thumping -- something large, somewhere deep, hitting a wall. Over and over.

---

### Episode 3: Die, Fuhrer, Die!

**Environmental Details**:
- **Berlin streets**: Bombed-out buildings, rubble, fires. Propaganda posters peeling from walls -- "ENDSIEG" (Final Victory), increasingly desperate. Civilian belongings scattered: a child's toy, a shattered photograph, a suitcase.
- **Bunker entrance**: The transition from ruined Berlin to the clean, bright bunker interior is jarring. Underground, the war doesn't exist. Everything is polished concrete and humming ventilation.
- **Military command rooms**: Maps with miniature flags showing troop positions. The flags are being moved backward -- the Allies are winning. Officers' ashtrays are overflowing. Coffee cups are stacked. They have been working without sleep.
- **Hitler's personal quarters**: Grotesque luxury underground. Original paintings (stolen from museums) on concrete walls. Fine furniture bolted to the floor (bunker precaution). A gramophone playing Wagner. A desk covered in medications -- the historical detail of Hitler's drug regimen subtly represented.
- **The mech suit chamber**: A vast, empty room designed for one purpose. Scorch marks on the floor from weapons testing. Tool racks, welding equipment, schematics pinned to the walls showing the Panzerfuhrer suit from every angle.

**Secret Rooms**:
- Eva's room: A small, lavishly decorated bedroom with personal items. A photograph of Hitler, a cyanide capsule in a jewelry box. Telling a silent story.
- A communications room with decoded Allied transmissions. The Nazis know more about Allied plans than the Allies realize. Among the intercepts: a document about B.J.'s mission, marked "LET HIM COME."

**Audio Cues**:
- Surface: bombers, anti-aircraft, explosions (muffled as the player descends)
- Bunker: the hum of generators, distant footsteps, the occasional PA announcement in German
- Hitler's wing: Wagner on the gramophone, growing louder as the player approaches
- The mech suit chamber: silence, then hydraulic startup sounds

---

### Episode 4: A Dark Secret

**Environmental Details**:
- **Drainage tunnels**: Medieval stonework, moss, running water. Scratched marks from B.J.'s entrance route. A sense of descending into the castle's hidden guts.
- **Chemical storage**: Yellow warning signs, hazmat symbols, sealed barrels arranged with military precision. Some barrels are leaking -- green puddles on the floor that damage the player on contact.
- **Testing chambers**: The worst room in the game. Observation windows. Chairs with restraints. A whiteboard with exposure time calculations. Photographs pinned to a corkboard showing the progression of symptoms -- each photograph more horrifying than the last. (Kept abstract/implied, never explicit.)
- **Giftmacher's office**: Immaculate. Every pen aligned. Every document filed. A periodic table on the wall with certain elements circled in red. A framed diploma (his medical degree, revoked). A single personal item: a photograph of a woman and child. His family. The horror of a monster who goes home at night.

**Secret Rooms**:
- A sealed testing chamber containing the remains of the "first batch" -- gas masks that failed, scattered personal effects. Dog tags identify them as Allied POWs.
- Giftmacher's private workshop where he experiments on his own formulas. A journal detailing his "improvements" with the clinical pride of an artist.

---

### Episode 5: Trail of the Madman

**Environmental Details**:
- **Forest approach**: Warning signs, barbed wire, cleared sight lines. The forest has been militarized -- trees cut to prevent cover, mines buried along footpaths.
- **Castle Erlangen exterior**: Modern fortifications over medieval walls. Radar dishes, radio antennae, motor pool with armored vehicles. This is a working military installation.
- **Gretel's command center**: A map room with pins tracking B.J.'s known movements across Europe. Photographs of every OSA agent she has identified. Red X marks through three of them -- confirmed kills. B.J.'s photograph is at the center, unmarked. She wants him alive. She wants to beat him face to face.
- **The dossier wall**: An entire room dedicated to B.J. Blazkowicz. His childhood. His military record. Interviews with captured OSA agents about his methods. Psychological profile (labeled "Diagnosis: Unstoppable"). It is flattering and terrifying in equal measure.

**Secret Rooms**:
- Hans Grosse's memorial: A small room containing Hans's personal effects, returned to Gretel after his death. His chain guns (a second pair), his medals, a photograph of both siblings as children. Gretel has placed flowers. It is the only human moment in her castle.
- An intercepted OSA communication revealing the identity of the double agent who betrayed B.J. in Episode 4. The double agent was one of Gretel's assets.

---

### Episode 6: Confrontation

**Environmental Details**:
- **Mountain exterior**: Gun emplacements carved into rock, armored doors, observation posts. The scale is overwhelming -- this is a fortress designed to survive the end of the world.
- **Industrial complex**: Assembly lines producing weapons, ammunition, vehicles. Workers (forced laborers, implied by their appearance and conditions) move in silent rows. The machinery never stops.
- **Research wing**: A continuation of Schabbs' work under new scientists. Updated equipment, advanced mutants in containment. Notes reference "Phase 2" and "the General's deadline." The doomsday weapon components are visible -- massive canisters connected to a delivery system.
- **Fettgesicht's office suite**: Excessive luxury -- art, wine, cigars, a personal chef's kitchen. While the forced laborers work in the factories below, Fettgesicht lives like a king. The contrast is the point.
- **The doomsday folder**: On Fettgesicht's desk after the fight. B.J. picks it up. Inside: deployment plans, target cities, casualty projections. Hundreds of thousands. This is what B.J. prevented.

**Secret Rooms**:
- A hidden archive containing documentation of all Nazi secret programs -- not just Eisenfaust and the chemical weapons, but others B.J. has never encountered. Programs with codenames like "Schwarze Sonne" (Black Sun) and "Das Grab" (The Grave). Hints that the horror goes deeper than even B.J. imagined. (Setup for potential sequel content.)
- A forced laborer's hidden message, scratched into a wall behind a loose panel: "If someone finds this -- we are 200. They work us until we die. Tell the world. Tell them we were here." B.J. reads it. His jaw tightens. He photographs it.

## 5. Onboarding / Tutorial

### Design Philosophy

The tutorial is invisible. The player should never feel "taught." Instead, Episode 1, Level 1 is designed as a funnel that naturally introduces each mechanic in a low-risk environment before demanding mastery.

### Episode 1, Level 1: "The Cell"

**Section 1: Movement (first 10 seconds)**
- B.J. starts in a cell. The door is open (the intro cinematic showed him taking down the guard).
- The corridor ahead has only one direction. The player learns WASD/arrow movement by simply exploring.
- A visual prompt appears if no input is detected for 5 seconds: "WASD to move. Mouse to look."
- The corridor turns. The player learns mouselook.

**Section 2: First Weapon (30 seconds in)**
- The dead guard's body lies at the first turn. A pistol glints on the floor.
- Walking over it triggers pickup. HUD element appears: pistol icon, ammo count.
- A display text fades in and out: "Press [1] for knife, [2] for pistol."
- An interactive prompt demonstrates the weapon switch.

**Section 3: First Combat (45 seconds in)**
- One guard ahead, back turned, at the end of a short corridor. No escape route for the player. This fight MUST happen.
- The guard is stationary, giving the player time to aim and fire.
- After the kill, the guard drops ammo and health. The player learns that enemies drop items.
- If the player uses the knife instead of the pistol, the guard dies in one hit from behind. The player learns stealth kills are effective.

**Section 4: First Door (60 seconds in)**
- A closed door blocks the path. Walking into it opens it.
- Brief text: "Walk into doors to open them."
- Behind the door: a room with health, ammo, and the first set of two diverging paths. The player learns that exploration is rewarded.

**Section 5: First Secret (90 seconds in)**
- One of the walls in this room has a slightly different texture (more worn, a hairline crack).
- If the player pushes against it, it slides open, revealing a treasure room.
- A brief, subtle sound cue (stone sliding) teaches the player to listen for secret indicators.
- If the player misses it, no penalty. The game continues. They will find secrets naturally as they become more observant.

**Section 6: Escalation (2 minutes in)**
- Two guards in the next room, both alert. The player must handle multiple enemies.
- The room has columns for cover. The player learns movement-based combat.
- After this fight, the level opens up into a more complex layout. The tutorial is over. The game has begun.

### Difficulty Selection Screen

**Screen Design**: B.J. stands in profile, looking at the camera. Four difficulty options appear, and B.J.'s expression and pose change for each:

| Difficulty | Name | B.J.'s Reaction |
|------------|------|-----------------|
| Easy | "Can I play, Daddy?" | B.J. in a baby bonnet, sucking a pacifier (classic Wolf3D reference) |
| Normal | "Don't hurt me." | B.J. looks nervous, holds pistol uncertainly |
| Hard | "Bring 'em on!" | B.J. grins, cracks knuckles, confident |
| Ultra | "I am Death incarnate!" | B.J.'s face is cast in shadow, eyes glowing, weapons bristling |

**B.J. Voice Lines per difficulty** (played when selected):
- Easy: "We all start somewhere." (Encouraging, no judgment)
- Normal: "This is where most soldiers fight. Good enough for me."
- Hard: "Now we're talking."
- Ultra: *No words. Just the sound of a magazine being loaded, a slide being racked, and a single, quiet exhale.*

## 7. Tone Bible

### The Balance Between Action and Horror

Wolfenstein 3D is fundamentally an **action game with horror elements**, not a horror game with action elements. The player should feel **powerful** -- B.J. is a one-man army. But that power should be earned, and the things he fights against should be genuinely unsettling.

**The Horror Spectrum by Episode**:

| Episode | Action/Horror Ratio | Primary Tone |
|---------|---------------------|--------------|
| 1 | 70/30 | Survival thriller with horror atmosphere |
| 2 | 40/60 | Full horror -- this is the scary episode |
| 3 | 90/10 | Pure action spectacle |
| 4 | 50/50 | Espionage thriller with chemical horror |
| 5 | 80/20 | Action thriller with psychological tension |
| 6 | 85/15 | Apocalyptic action with horror callbacks |

**Rules for Horror Moments**:
- Horror comes from **implication**, not explicitness. What the player imagines is always worse than what we show.
- Environmental horror (bloodstains, restraint chairs, medical logs) is more effective than jump scares.
- The mutants are the game's horror anchor. Their design should be unsettling, their sounds disturbing, their movement unnatural. They were people. The player should never forget that.
- Give horror moments room to breathe. Don't follow a disturbing discovery with an immediate action sequence. Let the player sit with it for a moment.

**Rules for Action Moments**:
- Action should feel **cathartic**. After a tense sequence, let the player unleash. Give them a chain gun and a room full of enemies.
- Boss fights are spectacle -- big, loud, cinematic. The player should feel like an action hero.
- Combat encounters should have rhythm: tension, burst, release. Not constant noise.

---

### Handling the Nazi Theme

This game is set during World War II and features Nazi enemies. This subject matter demands careful, responsible treatment.

**Core Principles**:

1. **Historical Context, Not Glorification**
   - The Nazis are the villains. Unambiguously. Completely. There is no moral ambiguity about the enemy.
   - Nazi iconography (swastikas, SS runes, eagles) is used to identify the enemy and establish setting, not to glorify or aestheticize.
   - The environmental storytelling shows the consequences of Nazi ideology: the labs, the chemical weapons, the prisoners. The game does not shy away from what the Nazis did, but it presents it as horror, not spectacle.

2. **The Humanity of Enemies**
   - Regular guards are human. Their death cries, their personal effects in barracks, the family photos on their desks -- these remind the player that soldiers are people. This does not excuse them. It complicates the action in a mature, meaningful way.
   - The bosses are dehumanized by their own choices. Hans chose cruelty. Schabbs chose amorality. Giftmacher chose zealotry. They are monsters because of what they decided to become, not because they were born that way.

3. **The Weight of Violence**
   - B.J. does not enjoy killing. He is efficient, determined, and relentless, but he takes no pleasure in it. His rare comments about enemies are grim, not gleeful.
   - The game acknowledges the toll. B.J.'s voice-over narration in cinematics shows a man who carries the weight of what he's done and seen. He fights because he must, not because he wants to.
   - Victory cinematics are not celebrations -- they are moments of exhaustion, resolve, and the grim satisfaction of having stopped something terrible.

4. **What We Don't Show**
   - No concentration camp levels. The game acknowledges their existence through documents and environmental cues but does not make them playable content. This is a line we do not cross.
   - No torture of helpless characters on-screen. We imply it through environment. The player finds the aftermath, not the act.
   - No civilian casualties caused by the player. Combat is exclusively against armed military personnel and mutant enemies.

---

### Intensity Pacing

The game cannot sustain maximum intensity for its entire duration. The player needs moments to breathe, explore, and absorb the story.

**The Intensity Wave Pattern**:

```
Level start: LOW (exploration, environmental storytelling)
     |
     v
First encounters: MEDIUM (combat ramps up)
     |
     v
Mid-level: HIGH (intense combat, multiple enemies)
     |
     v
Quiet room: LOW (secret discovery, story element, health/ammo cache)
     |
     v
Pre-boss approach: RISING (music intensifies, enemy density increases)
     |
     v
Boss fight: MAXIMUM
     |
     v
Post-boss: ZERO (cinematic, relief)
```

**Breathing Room Design**:
- Every 3-4 intense rooms, include a "quiet room" -- a space with minimal enemies, environmental storytelling, and supply pickups
- Secret rooms are always calm -- finding a secret is its own reward; don't punish exploration with ambushes
- Between episodes, the cinematics provide a complete reset -- the player can decompress before the next chapter

**Music's Role in Pacing**:
- Exploration: Ambient, atmospheric, low-key
- Combat: Driving, rhythmic, metal-influenced (honoring the original Bobby Prince soundtrack)
- Horror moments: Minimal, discordant, unsettling
- Boss fights: Full orchestral/metal hybrid, unique theme per boss
- Post-boss: Silence, then a slow, resolving melody

---

### The Role of Humor

Wolfenstein 3D has always balanced intense action with moments of absurdity. The original game had Mech-Hitler, dog food as health pickups, and the "Mein Leben!" death cry. This remake honors that tradition.

**Where Humor Lives**:
- **B.J.'s dry one-liners**: Understated, situational, never breaking tension completely. "One gun. That's all I've ever needed." He is not a comedian. He is a man coping.
- **The difficulty screen**: The classic baby-bonnet image is a beloved tradition. Preserve it.
- **Loading screen tips**: Self-aware, meta, occasionally absurd. These exist outside the fiction and can break the fourth wall.
- **Easter egg rooms**: The Pac-Man level, the Commander Keen room, the Doom helmet -- these are moments of levity within the game world that reward exploration.
- **Boss absurdity**: Mech-Hitler is inherently ridiculous. Lean into it. The game takes the THREAT seriously while acknowledging that a man in a four-chain-gun robot suit is bonkers. Hitler's over-the-top screaming contrasts with B.J.'s quiet determination.
- **Enemy death cries**: "Mein Leben!" is funny. It was always funny. Keep it.

**Where Humor Does NOT Live**:
- The horror sequences (Episode 2 especially). The mutants, the labs, the experiments -- none of this is played for laughs.
- The environmental storytelling about victims. The prisoner cells, the testing chambers, the forced laborers. This is treated with gravity.
- B.J.'s voice-over narration in cinematics. These are his most honest moments and are played straight.
- The final game ending. The sunrise scene is earned, and it is genuine.
